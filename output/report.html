Hyperscaling · The AI Buildout

The $424 Billion Bet

Seven American companies are spending more on data centers than the country invests in most of its industries. The wager is that demand for artificial intelligence will arrive to justify it. The costs of the buildout are already moving onto the power grid — unevenly, and toward the places least able to refuse them.

By Michael Nolan
May 15, 2026

In 2025, seven companies — Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Oracle, Nvidia and CoreWeave — committed about $424 billion to capital investment, most of it data centers built for artificial intelligence. That single year’s spending equals roughly 1.4 percent of American gross domestic product, close to a tenth of all private business investment in the country, and nearly a third — 31 percent — of everything American business spends on information-technology equipment and software combined.

A decade earlier, the same seven firms spent on the order of $0 billion a year between them. The path from there to here is not a gentle trend. The total roughly doubled in the single year between 2024 and 2025.

The Scale

Numbers this large invite double-counting, and most published tallies of “AI investment” commit it. The $424 billion is real investment — money spent, and assets leased, to build and equip data centers. It is a distinct quantity from the $259 billion of venture capital that flowed into AI companies worldwide in 2025, which is a financing flow, not a building. And it is distinct again from the $405 billion in revenue earned last year by the five largest US-listed chip suppliers — a figure not added to the buildout but contained within it, since those chips are part of what the capital spending buys.

What the three figures share is concentration. Nvidia alone booked $216 billion in revenue, 53 percent of the five-firm chip total. Seven companies account for the capital. The AI economy, measured in dollars, is not an economy of many participants. It is a handful of balance sheets, and the rest of the economy is increasingly downstream of them.

The most telling of those ratios is the last. When seven firms direct an amount equal to 31 percent of all national IT-equipment-and-software investment toward one category of asset, the buildout has stopped being a sector story. It is a claim on the economy’s investable surplus — capital that is, by definition, not going somewhere else.

And $424 billion is itself a floor. The figure counts cash spending and the assets the firms take on through finance leases, but not capacity rented through ordinary operating leases, nor the web of off-balance-sheet vehicles and private-credit arrangements that increasingly fund the buildout. Several of the largest data-center projects are now carried through joint ventures whose debt never lands on a hyperscaler’s balance sheet at all. The visible commitment is enormous; the full one is larger, and, by the way it is structured, meant to be harder to see.

Concentration on this scale is itself a kind of risk. When seven firms account for nearly a third of national IT investment, the economy’s growth, its stock-market gains and a measurable slice of its productivity statistics all become correlated with the same handful of decisions. The buildout is not only large. It has made the wider economy unusually dependent on whether a few boardrooms keep believing in it — and unusually exposed to the moment any of them stops.

The Chip Chokepoint

The buildout’s pace is not set by money alone. It is set by chips. The graphics processors that fill the data centers come, overwhelmingly, from one company: Nvidia’s data-center revenue dwarfs that of AMD, Broadcom, Micron and Intel combined. A single vendor’s production schedule — and the Taiwanese foundry that manufactures for it — is therefore a governor on the entire cycle.

That concentration is also where the buildout meets geopolitics. Advanced AI chips are subject to US export controls; the supply chain runs through a small number of firms and a smaller number of countries. The “hyperscaling” the rest of this report measures in dollars rests on a physical bottleneck that no amount of capital can widen quickly.

Nearly all of those chips are fabricated by a single contractor, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, on an island within reach of Chinese military pressure. A new leading-edge fabrication plant takes years and tens of billions of dollars to bring online. So the buildout carries a contradiction at its core: a financial commitment that can be made in a quarter depends on a manufacturing base that takes a decade to expand — and on a geography no balance sheet controls. The cycle can be funded far faster than it can be supplied.

The Bet

The spending is a wager on demand that has not yet arrived. Extending the seven firms’ own trajectory forward under a range of assumptions produces a cumulative 2026–2030 capital outlay of between $2.8 trillion and $6.8 trillion, with a central estimate near $4.6 trillion. Industry forecasters, counting the whole world and including chips and power, reach higher: McKinsey puts global data-center capital expenditure at $6.7 trillion through 2030; Goldman Sachs estimates $7.6 trillion of global AI capital spending through 2031.

The projection is deliberately conservative. The seven firms’ combined investment grew 71 percent between 2024 and 2025; even the high scenario assumes that pace decelerates every year through 2030, and the low scenario assumes it stalls. The range is wide because the quantity being projected is not really a trend. It is a decision the firms remake, and defend to their shareholders, every quarter.

Forecasts at this scale rest on assumptions that do not hold still. Goldman describes its own numbers as baseline estimates that are “extremely sensitive.” The harder question is revenue. Bain & Company estimates that paying for the buildout on its current path would require roughly $2 trillion a year in new revenue by 2030, and projects the industry will fall some $800 billion short.

A forward commitment of several trillion dollars is being made against a revenue base that, on the industry’s own arithmetic, does not yet exist.

This gap is what the underlying research frames as a geopolitically backstopped investment cycle: the spending continues because it is treated, by the firms and their investors, as too important to be allowed to fail. The data can measure the gap a backstop would have to fill. It cannot show that the backstop exists. That remains a thesis, not a finding.

The shape of the wager is not new. Britain’s railway mania of the 1840s and America’s late-1990s fiber-optic glut both funneled a society’s savings into infrastructure built well ahead of demand. Both left two things behind: capacity that the economy eventually grew into, and a generation of investors who were ruined before it did. The underlying research treats the AI buildout as the same species of event — a churn — and the unsettled question is simply which half of that inheritance the present cycle is accumulating.

For the hopeful half to win, a great deal has to come true at once: that AI applications earn revenue at the scale the spending implies, that the chips bought this year are not obsolete before they are paid off, and that the power and the permits arrive on schedule. Each is plausible on its own. The wager is that all of them hold together — and that joint probability, more than any single line in a forecast, is what the models cannot honestly price.

The Grid

Whatever the buildout’s eventual returns, its first bills are already due, and they are arriving somewhere other than the seven firms’ income statements. The most immediate is electricity. For thirteen years, from 2007 to 2020, total US electricity demand was essentially flat — a generation of efficiency gains canceling out growth.

For most of those years the explanation was benign. American electricity demand stopped growing because the economy kept learning to do more with less power — efficient lighting, better motors, and a manufacturing base that had shifted much of its heaviest load offshore. Forecasters came to treat flat demand as the natural condition of a mature grid, and two decades of utility planning, transmission investment and rate design were built on that assumption. The data-center surge is the first force in a generation strong enough to break it.

That era is over. Demand is now rising about 1.8 percent a year, and data centers are a principal reason. They consumed roughly 4.4 percent of US electricity in 2023; depending on the scenario, that share is projected to reach between 9 and 17 percent by 2030. A grid that was planned around stability is being asked to absorb a step-change.

Electricity is not like other inputs. It cannot be meaningfully stored at grid scale; the transmission lines to move it take the better part of a decade to permit and build; and a data center signs contracts for power years before the supply to serve it exists. A demand shock that another commodity would absorb through inventory and trade arrives in the power system as something harder: interconnection queues, capacity shortfalls, and price. The grid cannot be scaled at the speed of capital, and the buildout is testing exactly that mismatch.

Where the Load Lands

A national average of 4.4 percent is a misleading number, because the load does not spread evenly. It clusters. Virginia alone hosts an estimated 32 terawatt-hours of data-center demand — on the order of a quarter of the entire state’s electricity. The corridor through Loudoun County is known in the industry, without irony, as “Data Center Alley.”

Texas follows at roughly 17 terawatt-hours, Illinois at 12, with California and Oregon close behind. By EPRI’s estimate, about fifteen states account for some 80 percent of all US data-center electricity. The buildout is a national story told in a few ZIP codes — and those places carry a grid burden the national average never shows.

Virginia is the case that shows what concentration means. The state’s dominant utility, Dominion Energy, has reported data-center connection requests running into the tens of gigawatts — on the order of several times the entire state’s current peak demand. Much of that queue is speculative and will never be built; interconnection lists are padded with projects that hedge their bets. But even a fraction of it implies a state, and a set of counties, whose grid, land use and politics are being reorganized around server farms inside a single decade. The buildout does not arrive everywhere. It arrives somewhere, all at once.

Concentration changes the politics, too. A cost spread thinly across a national average draws no opposition; the same cost landing on a single county draws town-hall fights, building moratoriums and ballot measures. The map of the buildout is therefore also a map of where its legitimacy will be contested — and, increasingly, where local governments are weighing the promised tax revenue against the load, the land and the water, and beginning, in some places, to say no.

The Price Question

Does the load raise prices? Our own comparison of state electricity data finds that residential power prices in the data-center-heavy states rose about 38 percent between 2020 and 2025, against roughly 28 percent elsewhere — and the two tracked together until about 2021, then diverged. Over the same window, electricity consumption in those states grew 13 percent, against 8 percent elsewhere; Virginia’s alone grew 23 percent.

The Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas reaches a sharper version of the same conclusion. Its dispatch model finds data centers have already raised US wholesale electricity prices by roughly 4 percent, and could raise them 20–50 percent by 2028 depending on how much of the announced load is built and how hard it runs. In the PJM grid region, capacity prices have already jumped roughly elevenfold in two auctions, with data centers blamed for most of the increase.

In the data-center states, residential power has risen by more than a third in five years. Whether the data centers caused it is the most contested question in the file.

That contest is real. The Institute for Energy Research, examining the same period, found no statistically significant relationship between data-center density and state electricity prices — and noted that lower-growth states actually saw larger price increases. State prices are driven by fuel mix and policy as much as by load, and five heavy states are a small sample. The honest reading: the correlation is visible and the timing lines up, but causation is not settled.

Where the mechanism does operate, it is not mysterious. A large new load bids for the same generation and the same transmission as everyone else; in the capacity markets that pay power plants to stay available, it lifts the price every utility must pay to guarantee supply — and that cost flows through to all customers, data center or household alike. A server farm that opens in a county does not build itself a separate electricity system. It joins the one the residents already share, and bids against them in it.

Regulators have begun to respond. Virginia has approved a distinct rate class for the largest data centers — an attempt to make the load carry more of the transmission and generation it requires, rather than socializing that cost across residential bills. Whether such rules hold, and whether other states copy them, is now an active political fight, conducted utility by utility and rate case by rate case. The buildout has become a question of who pays, decided in venues most voters have never heard of.

How Prices Are Modeled

Behind every projected price increase is a model. The EIA’s Electricity Market Module solves a least-cost hourly dispatch across twenty-five regions of the country, and for the first time treats data-center load as an explicit driver. The Dallas Fed’s approach is similar in spirit — an hour-by-hour, unit-by-unit simulation of which power plants run to meet demand, and at what cost.

The projections range widely because they hinge on assumptions that are themselves uncertain: how fast AI chips age, how quickly new generation and transmission come online, and how much of the load queued for connection actually materializes. The Dallas Fed estimates data centers add about 0.05 of a percentage point to consumer price inflation in 2026, rising toward 2030 — and potentially doubling if renewable build-out lags. Small numbers, spread across every household in the country.

The honesty of a forecast at this scale lies in its range. A model that returns one confident number is concealing its assumptions; one that returns a wide band is showing them. The gap between a 20 percent and a 50 percent wholesale-price impact is not analytical weakness — it is an accurate statement of how much still depends on choices not yet made: how fast chips are retired, how quickly generation and transmission are built, whether the queued load shows up at all. The forecasts disagree because the future genuinely does.

Data Centers as Power Players

If data centers strain the grid, can they also feed it — can they resell power? As of 2026, not as net sellers. But they are no longer passive loads either. Increasingly, they bring their own power. Meta has contracted 1,121 megawatts of Illinois nuclear capacity; Amazon has locked up 1,920 megawatts of Pennsylvania nuclear and is funding small modular reactors; Google has signed for the output of a restarted Iowa reactor.

Some skip the grid altogether. xAI’s “Colossus” site in Memphis runs largely off-grid on gas turbines and Tesla batteries. Others sell flexibility back: Google has committed around a gigawatt of demand response, agreeing to curtail load when the grid is stressed. The emerging model — “bring your own power” — lets a data center bypass the years-long interconnection queue, and blurs the line between a computing company and a utility.

That blurring has consequences for everyone left on the shared system. A data center that builds its own gas plant to skip a five-year interconnection wait also removes itself, in part, from the grid that pools costs and risks across all customers. The buildout is not only adding load; it is quietly drawing a second, private power system alongside the public one — dedicated generation for those who can afford to commission it, and the older, slower, shared grid for everyone else. Whether that bifurcation is efficient or corrosive is one of the real questions the buildout poses, and it is barely being asked.

The Cubicle

The buildout’s second cost lands in the labor market, and it lands differently than earlier automation did. By one widely used measure, the average US job has about 31 percent of its tasks exposed to large language models — work the technology could materially speed up. Some 23 percent of US employment sits in occupations where more than half of tasks are exposed.

Two features complicate the headline. The first is that exposure is not the same as use: a second measure, built from observed AI usage rather than predicted capability, puts the employment-weighted figure closer to 13 percent. The gap between what is possible and what is actually happening is the adoption runway still ahead.

Earlier waves of automation hit the factory floor. This one is concentrated in the cubicle.

The second feature is who is exposed. Earlier automation fell hardest on lower-wage, manual work. This one inverts the pattern: exposure rises with wage. The most exposed large occupations are customer-service representatives, sales representatives and administrative assistants — the white-collar core, and the interpretive, linguistic work the underlying paper identifies as the cultural capital now being absorbed.

Exposure is not displacement, and the report is careful not to let the two slide together. But the early signal is specific. The work most exposed — drafting a reply, summarizing a document, reconciling a ledger, writing a function — is the work that has long been the first rung of a white-collar career: the tasks juniors do while they learn the ones that cannot be written down. A labor market knows how to absorb the automation of tasks. It is far less clear how it absorbs the automation of the bottom rung of the ladder.

The two measures together also set the clock. Predicted exposure marks where the technology can eventually reach; observed use marks how far it has reached so far. The distance between roughly a third of tasks and roughly an eighth is the room remaining before the labor-market effects, whatever they turn out to be, are fully felt. That gap is closing — and on the evidence of the usage data, it is closing fastest in exactly the high-wage, white-collar work that the predicted measure flags as most exposed.

The Concrete

There is one sector where the buildout adds work rather than displacing it. Data-center construction has grown to 1.32 percent of all US construction spending — a fivefold rise in its share over a decade — and the construction workforce stands at 8.3 million. The concrete, steel and switchgear of the AI economy still require hands, even as the work done inside the finished buildings does not.

It is a revealing exception. The buildout’s lasting employment is in pouring the foundation, not in operating what stands on it: a finished hyperscale data center, for all the electricity it draws, is run by a few dozen technicians. The jobs the AI economy reliably creates are disproportionately the temporary ones — and they end when the concrete cures. What the buildout leaves behind is a structure that employs almost no one and consumes the power of a small city.

What the Numbers Cannot Say

Several limits are worth stating plainly. The $424 billion is not a pure AI figure; corporate filings do not separate AI spending from ordinary cloud investment. Task exposure measures what is possible, not a count of jobs lost. The link between data centers and electricity prices is a contested correlation, not a settled cause. Below the state level, the energy picture goes dark for lack of public data. And the central wager — that demand will arrive to justify the spending — is precisely what no number can yet settle.

None of this is a prediction of failure. The buildout may be vindicated; the demand may arrive; the data centers may yet earn back what they cost. The purpose of an accounting is narrower and more stubborn than a forecast. It is to set down clearly what has been spent, by whom, and who is already paying the bills that have come due — so that when the verdict on the wager finally lands, the ledger of who carried it in the meantime is not quietly lost.

What the numbers do establish is the size of the bet, the smallness of the group making it, and the fact that its costs have already begun to move outward — onto the grid, onto the wage structure, onto the allocation of national capital, and onto particular places that did not choose the buildout. That asymmetry, between costs that are present and returns that are still a forecast, is the defining feature of the AI buildout as of mid-2026.

How this was measured
