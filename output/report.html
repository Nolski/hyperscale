Hyperscaling · The AI Buildout

The $424 Billion Bet

Seven American companies are spending more on data centers than the country invests in most of its industries. The wager is that demand for artificial intelligence will arrive to justify it — and the costs of the buildout are already moving onto the power grid and into the labor market.

By Michael Nolan
May 15, 2026

In 2025, seven companies — Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Oracle, Nvidia and CoreWeave — committed about $424 billion to capital investment, most of it data centers built for artificial intelligence. That is roughly 1.4 percent of American gross domestic product, close to a tenth of all private business investment in the United States, and nearly a third — 31 percent — of everything American business spends on information-technology equipment and software combined.

A decade earlier, the same seven firms spent on the order of $0 billion a year between them. The path from there to here is not a gentle trend. The total roughly doubled in the single year between 2024 and 2025.

The Scale

Numbers this large invite double-counting, and most published tallies of “AI investment” commit it. The $424 billion is real investment — money spent, and assets leased, to build and equip data centers. It is distinct from the $259 billion of venture capital that flowed into AI companies worldwide in 2025 ($194 billion of it in the United States), which is a financing flow, not a building. And it is distinct again from the $405 billion in revenue earned last year by the five largest US-listed chip suppliers — a figure not added to the buildout but contained within it, since those chips are what the spending buys.

What the three figures share is concentration. Nvidia alone booked $216 billion in revenue, 53 percent of the five-firm total. Seven companies account for the capital. The AI economy, measured in dollars, is not an economy of many participants. It is a handful of balance sheets.

The most telling of those ratios is the last. When seven firms direct an amount equal to 31 percent of all national IT-equipment-and-software investment toward one category of asset, the buildout has stopped being a sector story. It is a claim on the economy's investable surplus.

Even $424 billion is a floor. The figure counts cash spending and the assets the firms lease through finance leases, but not capacity rented through ordinary operating leases, nor the off-balance-sheet vehicles and private-credit arrangements that increasingly fund data centers. The true scale of the commitment is larger than any single line in a financial statement — and, by design, harder to see.

The Bet

The spending is a wager on demand that has not yet arrived. Extending the seven firms' own trajectory forward under a range of assumptions produces a cumulative 2026–2030 capital outlay of between $2.8 trillion and $6.8 trillion, with a central estimate near $4.6 trillion. Industry forecasters, counting the whole world and including chips and power, reach higher: McKinsey puts global data-center capital expenditure at $6.7 trillion through 2030; Goldman Sachs estimates $7.6 trillion of global AI capital spending through 2031.

The projection is deliberately conservative. The seven firms' combined investment grew 71 percent between 2024 and 2025; even the high scenario here assumes that pace decelerates every year through 2030, and the low scenario assumes it stalls outright. The range is wide because the quantity being projected is not really a trend. It is a decision — one the firms remake, and defend to their shareholders, every quarter.

Forecasts at this scale rest on assumptions that do not hold still. Goldman describes its own numbers as baseline estimates that are “extremely sensitive” — a single change in how fast AI chips are assumed to age can move the total by hundreds of billions of dollars. The harder question is revenue. Bain & Company estimates that paying for the buildout on its current path would require roughly $2 trillion a year in new revenue by 2030, and projects the industry will fall some $800 billion short.

This gap is what the project's framing — of an AI buildout treated as a geopolitically backstopped investment cycle — is built to explain. The argument is that the spending continues because it is understood, by the firms and their investors, as too important to be allowed to fail. The data assembled here can measure the gap a backstop would have to fill. It cannot show that the backstop exists. That remains a thesis, not a finding.

The Churn

Whatever the buildout's eventual returns, its costs are already arriving — and they are arriving somewhere other than the seven firms' income statements.

Start with electricity. For thirteen years, from 2007 to 2020, total US electricity demand was essentially flat. It is now rising again, by about 1.8 percent a year, and data centers are a principal reason. They consumed roughly 4.4 percent of US electricity in 2023; depending on the scenario, that share is projected to reach between 9 and 17 percent by 2030.

Over the same quarter-century in which data-center demand climbed, the average American residential electricity price doubled, from 8.6 to 17.3 cents per kilowatt-hour. The data here establishes the coincidence, not the cause; prices rise for many reasons. But the distributional shape is plain. The load growth is corporate. A rising share of the bill is paid by households.

The flat years are the context that makes the present moment legible. Utilities spent two decades planning around stable demand; the data-center surge has arrived faster than transmission and generation can be built to meet it. The cost of closing that gap — new power plants, new lines, and the higher price of electricity itself — is spread across every customer on the grid, whether or not they have any use for artificial intelligence.

Earlier waves of automation hit the factory floor. This one is concentrated in the cubicle.

The second cost lands in the labor market, and it lands differently than earlier automation did. By one widely used measure, the average US job has about 31 percent of its tasks exposed to large language models — work the technology could materially speed up. Some 23 percent of US employment sits in occupations where more than half of tasks are exposed.

Two features complicate the headline. The first is that exposure is not the same as use: a second measure, built from observed AI usage rather than predicted capability, puts the employment-weighted figure closer to 13 percent. The gap between the two — what is possible and what is actually happening — is the adoption runway still ahead.

The second feature is who is exposed. Earlier waves of automation fell hardest on lower-wage, manual work. This one inverts the pattern: exposure rises with wage. The most exposed large occupations are customer-service representatives, sales representatives and administrative assistants — the white-collar core.

The pattern is sharp at the level of individual jobs. Customer-service representatives — 2.7 million workers — score near 0.70 on the predicted measure and, unusually, near 0.70 on the observed one as well: a rare case in which what AI could do and what it is already doing agree. Sales representatives, general office clerks and accountants follow close behind. These are not marginal occupations. They are among the largest in the country, and they are the linguistic, interpretive work — the drafting, the answering, the reconciling — that the paper's argument identifies as the cultural capital now being absorbed.

There is one sector where the buildout adds work rather than displacing it. Data-center construction has grown to 1.32 percent of all US construction spending, and the construction workforce stands at 8.3 million. The concrete and steel of the AI economy still require hands.

What the Numbers Cannot Say

Several limits are worth stating plainly. The $424 billion is not a pure AI figure; corporate filings do not separate AI spending from ordinary cloud investment. Task exposure measures what is possible, not a count of jobs lost. The rise in electricity prices is a correlation, not a proven consequence. And the central wager — that demand will arrive to justify the spending — is precisely what the numbers cannot yet settle.

What they do establish is the size of the bet, the smallness of the group making it, and the fact that its costs have already begun to move outward — onto the grid, onto the wage structure, onto the allocation of national capital — while its returns remain a forecast. That asymmetry, between costs that are present and returns that are promised, is the buildout's defining feature as of mid-2026.
